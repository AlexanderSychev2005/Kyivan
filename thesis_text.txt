1. Introduction 
The computational study of historical written documents sits at the intersection of 
epigraphy, computer vision, and computational linguistics. The subject of the present work is the 
Novgorodian birchbark manuscripts — documents scratched on pieces of birchbark that have 
been discovered, and continue to be discovered, in the vicinity of Veliky Novgorod and Staraya 
Russa, as well as a number of other locations across medieval Rus'. 
The majority of manuscripts is written in the Old Novgorodian dialect (OND), which 
differs substantially from both standard Old Russian and Church Slavonic. The corpus of direct 
OND sources is considerably small. It includes, in addition to the birchbark manuscripts 
themselves, a number of epigraphic inscriptions on objects, some marginal notes in ecclesiastical 
books, and, with certain qualifications, a portion of Novgorodian parchment and paper charters 
in which dialectal features predominate over supradialectal norms. Indirect sources — oriented 
primarily towards Church Slavonic norms but containing Novgorodian features — encompass 
the remaining Novgorodian parchment and paper charters, the Novgorodian chronicles 
(especially the First Novgorodian Chronicle), and ecclesiastical books (Zaliznyak, 2004: 11–13). 
Many birchbark manuscripts have survived only in fragmentary condition, making their 
restoration and interpretation a considerable challenge. Computational models can serve as 
valuable assistants in this process, providing additional evidence to support and complement the 
work of human scholars. The aim of the present work is to develop a pipeline for filling lacunae 
in birchbark manuscripts written in OND, as well as for the temporal dating and categorical 
attribution of manuscripts. The small size of the corpus and the distinctive features of the dialect 
make this task particularly challenging. 
In a longer-term perspective, the text restoration module described here could be 
integrated into a broader birchbark manuscript processing pipeline, also encompassing optical 
character recognition from photographs and the automatic segmentation of continuous 
handwritten text into words. 
2. Literature Review 
2.1 Birchbark Manuscripts and Related Work 
Before turning to the literature on automatic ancient text restoration, it is important to 
survey work that engages directly with the birchbark manuscript corpus or with the broader 
domain of Old Russian and Church Slavonic. Computational approaches to handwritten 
historical documents have grown considerably in recent years, with contributions covering 
optical character recognition, script identification, handwriting analysis, and authorship 
attribution. However, studies addressing Cyrillic sources remain comparatively rare, and tend to 

focus on documents from the 18th century onwards (Diao et al., 2025: 18–19). The works 
discussed below are therefore particularly noteworthy. 
2.1.1 Script Recognition and Palaeographic Dating 
Sidorov (2018) proposed a method for the automatic palaeographic dating of birchbark 
manuscripts based on letterform features. The system combines classical appearance modelling 
with convolutional neural networks, and outputs predictions via an ensemble of 171 support 
vector machines — one for each interval between the identified chronological periods. The 
method achieved a mean absolute error of 18.9 years, a figure the author reports as comparable 
to the accuracy of expert palaeographers. 
Malashin et al. (2025) addressed the complementary task of character recognition directly 
on birchbark images. Analysing the manuscript database, the authors identified the 33 most 
frequent letters and generated a large synthetic training dataset by rendering these characters in 
varied scripts with added noise to simulate the surface texture of real birchbark. Several YOLO 
(You Only Look Once) object detection models were trained and evaluated; following 
hyperparameter optimisation, YOLOv10 achieved the best result with a mean Average Precision 
(mAP) of 0.89. The authors identify the high level of noise and physical damage inherent to 
birchbark, as well as the inconsistent and sometimes extremely thin stroke widths, as the 
principal challenges of the task. 
Although character recognition is an important prerequisite for a fully automated 
pipeline, it should be noted that for the existing transcribed corpus, the more pressing challenge 
lies not in reading individual letters but in interpreting damaged passages and recovering lost 
text. The present work focuses precisely on this task. 
2.1.2 Textual and Dialectal Analysis 
Afanasev and Lyashevskaya (2025) applied corpus-based language distance measurement 
to study the diatopic variation of the Old Novgorodian dialect. Using FastText embeddings 
trained on character n-grams (length 3–6), which allow robust operation on noisy data and small 
corpora without the biases associated with pre-trained transformers, the authors conducted 
analysis at the level of 3-character shingles. UPGMA clustering confirmed the chronological 
heterogeneity of the dialect and identified three stable periods — 1020–1160, 1160–1220, and 
1280–1430 — within each of which the manuscripts show a high degree of linguistic 
homogeneity. 
Lendvai et al. (2023) adapted BERT for the classification of pre-modern Slavic texts 
along three parameters: manuscript identity, century of composition, and region of origin. Their 
approach uses domain-adaptive pre-training via unsupervised MLM fine-tuning on a historical 

corpus spanning nearly a thousand years. The results confirm that domain adaptation improves 
classification accuracy, enabling the model to capture subtle dialectal and chronological 
distinctions even from relatively small datasets. 
Lendvai et al. (2025) investigated automatic retrieval and alignment of parallel text 
passages across chronological and regional variants of Church Slavonic. The study compares 
multilingual semantic embeddings (LaBSE, SBERT) against character-level alignment 
algorithms under conditions of strong orthographic variation and OCR noise. Multilingual 
models such as LaBSE achieve high precision without domain-specific adaptation, though their 
performance is critically dependent on sentence segmentation quality. The Smith–Waterman 
algorithm proved competitive with neural approaches on heavily damaged or noisy texts. 
2.2 Word Segmentation in Ancient Texts 
Word segmentation — the task of identifying token boundaries in continuous text — is a 
prerequisite for a complete manuscript processing pipeline, since many historical documents, 
including most birchbark manuscripts, follow a scriptio continua convention without spaces 
between words. However, this task is typically not addressed in restoration-focused studies, 
which generally assume pre-tokenised, space-delimited input. 
Robert-Hayek and Eyharabide (2025) proposed a method for the automatic segmentation 
of ancient Greek and Latin texts written in scriptio continua, based on hierarchical transformers. 
Their two-level architecture uses a character-level model to predict word boundaries and a 
word-level model to evaluate the linguistic plausibility of the resulting sequences, refined via 
self-training. The system achieves an F1 score of up to 96%, demonstrating that hierarchical 
modelling — simultaneously attending to graphemic features and global semantic context — 
outperforms standard transformer-based approaches on this task. While the full segmentation 
pipeline is out of scope for the present project, these results inform the longer-term integration 
perspective described in the Introduction. 
2.3 Masked Language Modelling for Ancient Text Restoration 
Approaches to lacuna restoration can broadly be divided into two categories: fine-tuned 
masked language models, which adapt pre-trained BERT-like architectures, and task-specific 
models trained from scratch, which are designed and optimised specifically for ancient text 
restoration. 
2.3.1 Fine-tuned Masked Language Models 
Following the introduction of BERT, researchers observed that the MLM objective used 
for its pre-training is structurally identical to the task of restoring lacunae in historical texts: in 
both cases, a model must predict a missing token given its surrounding context. This observation 

has since motivated a growing body of work applying or adapting BERT-like models to ancient 
and medieval corpora. 
Lazar et al. (2021) were among the first to exploit this parallel systematically, applying it 
to the restoration of lacunae in transliterated Akkadian texts from the Oracc corpus. During 
preprocessing, editorial uncertainty markers and alternative readings were removed, and texts 
were provided with word-space delimiters. In training, 15% of tokens were masked at random. 
Each missing token — a single Akkadian logographic sign — was represented by the special 
symbol 'x'; because Akkadian signs may correspond to multiple subword tokens in BERT's 
vocabulary, the authors adapted the k-beam algorithm to produce multi-subtoken predictions. Of 
the models compared, multilingual mBERT fine-tuned on Akkadian achieved the strongest 
performance, with Hit@5 of 89% and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) of 83%. Notably, basic 
mBERT outperformed model pre-trained from scratch on Akkadian data, suggesting that broad 
multilingual pre-training may compensate for the absence of domain-specific pre-training. 
Baidya et al. (2025) applied a similar framework to Sanskrit, a low-resource ancient 
language with a substantially larger corpus than OND. Their dataset comprised over one million 
sentences; they used a standard BERT architecture with a custom tokeniser employing Byte Pair 
Encoding (BPE) to handle Sanskrit's complex morphology and Devanagari script. The model 
achieved Hit@10 of 70%, outperforming SanBERTa (60%), a pre-existing Sanskrit language 
model, with a Character Error Rate of 23%. 
Gu et al. (2024) proposed a more architecturally innovative approach targeting classical 
Chinese — a language written without word boundaries. The authors constructed two evaluation 
datasets: a short-text set (300 documents, segmented into 30–60 character fragments, yielding 
approximately 424,000 training and 47,000 test examples with single-word masking), and a 
long-text set (400 documents, segmented into 256–512 character fragments, with variable 
masking rates of 10%, 20%, 30%, and 40% to assess model performance under progressively 
sparser context). Their model ACTRM extends SikuRoBERTa — a pre-trained model for 
classical Chinese — with a phrasal embedding that aggregates features from neighbouring 
tokens, designed to compensate for the absence of spaces. The standard MLP prediction head is 
replaced with a Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (KAN), which offers more parameter-efficient 
approximation and trainable activation functions. ACTRM maintains its advantage over 
competitors across all masking rates on the long-text dataset, with the performance gap widening 
as the proportion of masked tokens grows: Hit@5 declines from 76% at a 10% masking rate to 
61.5% at 40%, with a corresponding increase in perplexity from 9% to 24%. 

2.3.2 Task-specific Models Trained from Scratch 
The approaches discussed above predict one token at a time. An alternative line of work 
instead predicts the entire missing sequence in a single pass. 
Assael et al. (2019) introduced Pythia, the first deep learning model for ancient text 
restoration, applied to ancient Greek epigraphy. The model was trained on PHI-ML, a cleaned 
and machine-actionable version of the PHI corpus comprising over 3.2 million words across 
approximately 35,000 inscriptions spanning the 7th century BCE to the 5th century CE. Missing 
characters are represented by '-' and characters to be predicted by '?'; the target sequence is 
sampled from a contiguous span of up to ten characters within a broader context window. The 
architecture is a character-level sequence-to-sequence model with a bidirectional LSTM encoder 
and an attention-based LSTM decoder. To better capture word-level context  the encoder 
concatenates character embeddings with embeddings of the words they belong to; partially 
damaged or out-of-vocabulary words are mapped to a shared unknown embedding. Predictions 
are returned as a ranked list of top-20 hypotheses via beam search. The best-performing variant 
achieved a character error rate of 30.1%, substantially outperforming expert epigraphists (57.3% 
CER), and the ground-truth restoration appeared within the top-20 hypotheses in 73.5% of cases. 
The authors additionally demonstrated that predictive performance increases monotonically with 
context length, peaking around 500 characters. 
Building on Pythia, the same research group subsequently developed Ithaca (Assael et al., 
2022), extending the framework in several important directions. Where Pythia focused solely on 
text restoration, Ithaca tackled three tasks simultaneously: textual restoration, geographical 
attribution, and chronological attribution. The model was trained on a substantially expanded 
dataset of approximately 3.8 million words across 78,000 inscriptions from the PHI corpus 
spanning the 7th century BCE to the 5th century CE. Architecturally, Ithaca replaces the LSTM 
encoder-decoder with a transformer torso consisting of eight stacked blocks with sparse 
multi-head attention, enabling more efficient handling of long sequences. As in Pythia, inputs 
combine character- and word-level representations, with damaged or unknown words rendered as 
'[unk]'. Physical damage was simulated using a geometrically distributed masking scheme with 
parameter P = 0.1 at the character level, and editorial restorations enclosed in square brackets 
were retained in the training data to maximise the amount of available text. Ithaca achieved a 
CER of 26.3% and a top-20 accuracy of 78.3%, substantially outperforming both Pythia and the 
expert historian baseline (59.6% CER). For geographical attribution, the model reached 70.8% 
top-1 and 82.1% top-3 accuracy across 84 regions. For chronological attribution, Ithaca's 
predictions fell within an average of 29.3 years of the ground-truth date intervals, with a median 
distance of only 3 years. 

The successor system Aeneas (Assael et al., 2025) extended this framework to Latin 
epigraphy, training on over 176,000 inscriptions from three epigraphic databases covering 
approximately 16 million characters. Architectural innovations include the replacement of the 
BigBird-based torso with a T5 transformer decoder augmented with Rotary Position Embeddings 
(RoPE), and the removal of the word-level representation layer, with the model operating 
exclusively at the character level. A ResNet-based visual module is integrated for the 
geographical attribution task. Aeneas is also the first model in this line of work to handle gaps of 
unknown length, denoted by a ‘#’ symbol, using an auxiliary prediction head and a 
non-sequential beam search that at each step either extends the gap by one character or closes it. 
On the Latin epigraphic test set, Aeneas achieved a CER of 40.5% with known restoration length 
and 66.1% for gaps of unknown length, alongside a top-20 accuracy of 46.5%. Geographical 
attribution reached 72.3% top-1 accuracy across 62 Roman provinces, and chronological 
attribution achieved an average distance of 13 years from ground-truth date ranges, with a 
median of zero years. 
Both the Ithaca and Aeneas papers confirm the combination of model predictions and 
expert scholar judgment consistently outperformed either working in isolation, establishing a 
compelling case for human–AI collaboration in the digital humanities. 
3. Data 
3.1 Dataset Sources 
The dataset used for model training comprises nearly 3.8 million word tokens and was 
assembled from a variety of sources, subsequently filtered and normalised (see Section 3.2). It 
can broadly be divided into two parts: texts that are related to the Old Novgorodian dialect to 
varying degrees, and all remaining texts. Each of these parts is discussed in more detail  below. 
 

 
Fig. 1. Dataset statistics 
 
3.1.1 Old Novgorodian Dialect 
The open corpus of birchbark manuscripts, hosted at the portal Drevnerusskie 
berestyanye gramoty (gramoty.ru, accessed on 07.04.2026), currently contains 1,258 birchbark 
and 2 lead documents totalling around 24,000 words, with a vocabulary exceeding 4,100 lexical 
items. The birchbark manuscripts are short texts, with a median length of 65 characters or 12 
words per document (Fig. 1). In practice, very short documents tend to carry too little contextual 
information to be useful for training or evaluation. We therefore retain only those documents 
exceeding 25 characters in length, yielding a working subset of 958 manuscripts with a total of 
over 21,000 words. 
 

Fig. 2. Distribution of birchbark manuscripts’ length and its median value 
 
In the dataset, each document is provided in two forms — in the original writing without 
spaces (scriptio continua) and with word boundaries marked by spaces. Reflecting the layout of 
the documents, texts are divided into lines and contain hyphenation marks. The texts also employ 
a system of editorial conventions: scribal errors are enclosed in curly braces {}, partial 
reconstructions of letters based on surviving traces are marked with square brackets [], full 
reconstructions supported only by contextual inference are indicated by round brackets (), a 
single hyphen - denotes an unrestorable gap of one character, and an ellipsis … denotes an 
unrestorable gap of unknown length. 
The majority of birchbark manuscripts are damaged to some degree: more than two thirds 
of the full corpus (879 documents) contain gaps that are either irrecoverable or have not yet been 
reconstructed. Only 258 manuscripts, totalling over 5,000 words, are either fully intact or carry 
only partial reconstructions. 
As shown in Figs. 2–3, the majority of documents originate from Novgorod (1,145 items) 
and the nearby town of Staraya Russa (58), with the remainder distributed across Torzhok, 
Smolensk, Pskov, Tver, Moscow, Zvenigorod Galitsky, Pereyaslavl Ryazansky (Ryazan), Staraya 
Ryazan, Vologda, Vitebsk, and Mstislavl. Most manuscripts from Novgorod, Staraya Russa, 
Torzhok, and Pskov constitute the main direct source of the Old Novgorodian dialect in a broad 
sense (Zaliznyak, 2004: 6–7). 
 
Fig. 3. Number of birchbark manusripts by city 
 

 
Fig. 4. Visualization of manuscripts’ origin on map 
 
As additional sources of the Old Novgorodian dialect, we use Novgorod (34,300 words) 
and Pskov (1,300) parchment charters and the First Novgorodian Chronicle (nearly 34,000 
words). These documents differ stylistically from most birchbark manuscripts, yet retain a 
number of OND features. The Old Russian epigraphic dataset hosted at the portal 
Drevnerusskaja epigraphika (epigraphica.ru, accessed 07.04.2026) is also a structurally and 
partly thematically comparable source: its editorial conventions follow the same system as those 
used in the birchbark corpus. However, epigraphic inscriptions conform to Old Novgorodian 
norms only to a limited degree, as the majority of wall inscriptions follow Church Slavonic 
conventions. Applying the same filtering procedure as for the epigraphic manuscripts, we 
reduced the combined set from 10,200 to 7,100 words. 
3.1.2 Other Sources 
Given the comparatively limited amount of data available in the OND subset, it was 
decided to compile a broader dataset drawing on documents written in other Old Russian 
dialects, Church Slavonic, and a number of closely related languages. For this purpose, both 
existing datasets (DIACU, TOROT, and a biblical corpus of the Ponomar Project) and 
independently crawled or compiled materials were used. For a more detailed description of all 
datasets, see Appendix 1. 

3.2 Birchbark Manusctipts’ Metadata 
Each birchbark manuscript in the dataset is accompanied by metadata. Of the available 
metadata, we use the conventional date (uslovnaya data) and the genre category as target 
variables for the auxiliary classifiers described in Section 4.3. 
The conventional date, as defined by (Yanin & Zaliznyak, 2000: 136), is assigned by 
mapping each manuscript onto one of the segments of a chronological scale spanning the second 
quarter of the 11th century to the mid-15th century. The scale is divided primarily into 20-year 
segments (with 25-year segments for the 11th century and occasional 10-year segments for the 
15th century). When the underlying stratigraphic interval is wide (40–60 years or more), the 
midpoint is taken as the basis for assignment, shifted towards any archaeologically preferred 
sub-interval where one exists; palaeographic, graphemic, or linguistic features may provide 
additional refinement. For a smaller number of manuscripts whose content permits content-based 
dating — through mentions of specific persons or events — the conventional date follows 
content rather than stratigraphy, and this applies even to manuscripts that lack a stratigraphic date 
altogether. The conventional date thus takes the form of a 20-year interval rather than a single 
year, reflecting the inherent uncertainty of archaeological dating. 
 
Fig. 5. Birchbark manuscripts conventional dating 
 

 
Fig. 6. Number of birchbark manusripts by category (genre) 
 
As shown in Figures 5–6, both the dating distribution and the category distribution are 
highly skewed. The majority of manuscripts cluster around the 12th and 14th centuries, while the 
dominant category is private letters, accounting for approximately 700 manuscripts. 
For the dating prediction task, we discretise the timeline into nine bins of 50 years each, 
spanning 1050–1499. Four manuscripts dated 1025–1050 fall outside this range; as their interval 
overlaps with the first bin, they are assigned to it based on the overlap between their date interval 
and the bin boundary. 
For the category prediction task, we merge the original fine-grained categories into four 
broader classes: letters (private letters), records (business records and official documents), 
religious (religious texts), and other (educational texts, folklore, varia, and content of uncertain 
nature). Mixed-category records (annotated with //) are excluded. Even after this consolidation, 
the class distribution remains imbalanced: letters — 696, records — 330, other — 171, religious 
— 45. This imbalance is addressed during training via weighted cross-entropy loss. 
3.3 Text Preprocessing and Normalisation 
During dataset processing, overlapping portions were removed to prevent data leakage: 
for instance, the birchbark manuscript corpus was excluded entirely from TOROT. A substantial 
number of documents from DIACU were also discarded due to poor OCR quality in the original 
dataset. 

When processing birchbark manuscripts and epigraphic texts, the contents of curly braces 
{} were removed to prevent the model from training on ungrammatical forms, while the contents 
of both square brackets [] and round brackets () were retained to provide the model with 
additional context (for a detailed description of the notation system, see Section 3.1.1). Line 
break markers are removed, and hyphenated word fragments split across lines are rejoined into a 
single token. 
In certain other source documents, angle brackets <> were used for editorial annotations; 
their contents were likewise removed. Other editorial abbreviations and marks not present in the 
original text were also stripped during preprocessing. 
All text was lowercased. Of the available punctuation, three symbols were retained: the 
middle dot ·, the colon :, and the cross +, the latter having been used during certain periods to 
mark the beginning of a birchbark document (Zaliznyak, 2004: 37). Some rare characters are 
deleted, some transformed to their invariants (see Appendix 2 for normalisation scheme). 
For models that require it, an additional normalisation step is applied to ensure 
compatibility with the model's built-in tokenizer (see Section 4.1). 
3.4 Masking Scheme and Set Construction 
Data are split into training, validation, and test sets in a 90/5/5 ratio. The test set is 
organised into two evaluation tracks: Test A for artificially generated gaps, and Test B for real 
lacunae based on editorial reconstructions. 
Most birchbark manuscripts fit comfortably within BERT's maximum input length of 512 
tokens. However, some documents — most notably those from other datasets — considerably 
exceed this limit. Long texts are therefore split into overlapping chunks of 256 tokens. 
All sources are partitioned into training, validation, and test subsets in a 90/5/5 ratio. The 
test set is organised into two evaluation tracks. Test A is constructed by applying the masking 
procedure described below to fully intact text fragments, creating a benchmark with known 
ground-truth answers. Test B consists of documents from the birchbark and epigraphic corpora 
that contain at least one editorial reconstruction enclosed in square or round brackets: editorial 
reconstructions enclosed in brackets are concealed, and the model is evaluated on its ability to 
reproduce them character by character. Evaluating on two separate tracks allows a distinction to 
be drawn between the model's general restoration capacity — measured on Test A — and its 
practical utility for manuscript scholarship — measured on Test B. 

Most birchbark letters fit comfortably within the maximum input length of 512 tokens. 
Documents that exceed this limit — primarily those drawn from larger chronicle sources — are 
split into non-overlapping chunks of 256 tokens. 
The masking probability of 8% is set to match the empirically observed reconstruction 
density in the birchbark corpus. In Test B, 7,130 characters out of 96,606 are subject to editorial 
reconstruction, yielding a reconstruction rate of 7.38%. Including the 1,120 gap tokens already 
present in the same texts raises the combined ratio of masked and unreadable material to 8.54%. 
An 8% masking rate therefore reflects the proportion of damaged content in the target domain 
rather than an arbitrary hyperparameter choice. This value is held constant across both modelling 
approaches, so that any difference in restoration quality can be attributed to architectural factors 
rather than to differences in training conditions. 
For BERT-based models, masking is applied using a span-based scheme with span 
lengths drawn from a geometric distribution with parameter p = 0.35. This produces 
predominantly short spans with a long tail, approximating the distribution of contiguous damage 
in historical documents. The overall masking budget is kept at 8% per sequence. The gap token, 
which marks genuinely unreadable positions already present in the training text, is excluded 
from the set of eligible mask targets: the model observes it as context but is never trained to 
predict it, preventing the model from learning to output [GAP] as a restoration. 
Models trained from scratch use a purpose-built collator that simulates physical 
document degradation through three complementary strategies. Span masking expands randomly 
selected positions into contiguous runs, with span lengths sampled uniformly from [1, 
max_span] (max_span is set to 3), reflecting the fact that physical damage tends to affect 
adjacent characters rather than isolated ones. Edge masking (applied with probability 0.10) 
selects a short span at the beginning or end of the sequence and masks it entirely, simulating the 
frayed or broken edges characteristic of birchbark letters. Gap augmentation (applied with 
probability 0.05) randomly replaces short runs of non-special tokens with the gap token #, 
training the model to restore characters in the presence of neighbouring unreadable sections — a 
situation common in heavily damaged documents. The aggregate masking rate across all three 
strategies is calibrated to remain at approximately 8%, consistent with the MLM setup. 
3.5 Data Augmentation 
For sampling balance purposes, all documents were divided into six thematic groups and 
assigned corresponding context tags: DAILY, LEGAL, CHURCH, LIT, EPIC, and SCIENCE. 
This grouping was not intended to be exhaustive or precise: assignments were based on the 
original characterisation of each source, and documents from sources covering multiple registers 

were assigned to the most representative group. The tags were used solely to ensure a more 
balanced sampling distribution during training and do not constitute a linguistically motivated 
classification. 
As shown in Fig. 5, religious texts are substantially overrepresented in the raw corpus. To 
address this imbalance, source-level weights were proposed for the training split: documents 
from underrepresented categories are sampled multiple times per epoch, while overrepresented 
sources are downweighted. The weights were calibrated to approximate the ordering DAILY ≈ 
LEGAL > CHURCH > LIT > EPIC > SCIENCE, reflecting the thematic distribution of the 
target domain — birchbark manuscripts — in which everyday correspondence and legal 
documents constitute the majority of surviving texts.

4. Methods 
A fundamental methodological question in computational lacuna restoration concerns the 
granularity of the prediction unit. Ithaca predicts sequences of individual characters via beam 
search, requiring the number of missing characters to be known in advance. Aeneas operates at 
the subword token level and additionally introduces a mechanism for inferring the number of 
missing tokens when it is unknown. The choice of granularity has consequences for both 
evaluation and generalisation: character-level prediction aligns naturally with the editorial 
markup of birchbark manuscripts, where lacunae are defined at the character level, while 
token-level prediction allows the model to leverage subword morphological structure, which 
may be advantageous for a highly inflected language. 
Another significant issue is about whether restoration is performed position-by-position 
— predicting each masked unit independently — or jointly over the entire lacuna span via beam 
search. Independent per-position prediction is computationally cheap but ignores dependencies 
between the predicted units within a span; beam search is more expensive but allows the model 
to consider the joint probability of the restored sequence. 
We evaluate two families of approaches along both dimensions discussed above. 
Fine-tuned models (Section 4.1) are evaluated in two modes: character-level per-position 
prediction, where outputs are restricted to single Cyrillic characters via constrained decoding, 
and token-level per-position prediction, where the full subword vocabulary is available. This 
dual evaluation allows us to isolate the contribution of prediction granularity from the 
contribution of domain fine-tuning, using the same model weights in both modes. From-scratch 
models (Section 4.2) are designed to support sequence-level beam search: RoFormer operates at 
the BPE token level, while DualEmbLM uses character-level tokenisation natively. The 
comparison between RoFormer and DualEmbLM, both trained from scratch on the same corpus, 
isolates the effect of tokenisation granularity on restoration quality, holding all other architectural 
and training choices constant. 
4.1 Fine-tuned Models 
Three pre-trained transformer encoders are selected for fine-tuning: 
●​ mBERT (Devlin et al., 2018), [1] — a multilingual BERT model trained on 104 
languages using a shared WordPiece vocabulary. 
●​ BERTislav [2] — a ruBERT-based model pre-trained on a 10M-token corpus of Old 
Church Slavonic, Later Church Slavonic, Old East Slavic, Middle Russian, and Medieval 
Serbian texts. 

●​ ModernBERT (Warner et al., 2024), [3] — a bidirectional encoder with an extended 
context window and rotary positional embeddings. 
mBERT is included as a baseline because of its promising results on Akkadian lacuna 
restoration in Lazar et al. (2021). BERTislav is chosen because its pre-training domain partially 
overlaps with the target language variety. ModernBERT represents recent architectural 
improvements to the bidirectional encoder paradigm and serves as a more capable 
general-purpose baseline. All three models are fine-tuned on the assembled corpus using the 
MLM objective.

4.2 Models Trained from Scratch 
4.2.1 RoFormer 
The first from-scratch architecture is a RoFormer encoder (Su et al., 2021) trained from 
scratch on the assembled corpus using a BPE tokeniser with a vocabulary of 50,000 tokens. 
RoFormer replaces the absolute positional embeddings of standard BERT with rotary position 
embeddings applied to the query and key projections of each attention head, which has been 
shown to improve generalisation on longer sequences. The model follows a standard masked 
language modelling pre-training objective with the physical degradation collator described in 
Section 3.4. The architecture comprises 6 transformer layers, a hidden size of 512, 8 attention 
heads, and an intermediate feed-forward size of 2,048, yielding approximately 37M parameters 
in total. 
Since the BPE tokeniser does not guarantee that token boundaries coincide with the 
boundaries of editorial reconstructions, masking on Test B is extended to the nearest token 
boundaries. As a result, tokens that partially overlap with the lacuna boundary are masked in full, 
causing the model to lose access to characters that lie outside the bracketed span but within the 
same token. This effect is deterministic and reproducible for a fixed tokeniser, and constitutes a 
mild disadvantage relative to the character-level models, which mask exactly the characters 
specified by the editorial markup. 
4.2.2 DualEmbLM 
The second from-scratch architecture addresses the granularity mismatch identified in 
Section 4.2.1 by operating directly at the character level. DualEmbLM uses a custom character 
vocabulary constructed from the training corpus and a word-level vocabulary of 50,000 entries, 
combining both via dual embeddings: each input position receives a concatenation of its 
character embedding and the embedding of the word to which it belongs, projected into the 
shared hidden space. This design draws on the intuition underlying Ithaca (Assael et al., 2022), 
where character-level representations are enriched with broader lexical context. The transformer 
encoder follows the same configuration as RoFormer: 6 layers, hidden size 512, 8 attention 
heads, intermediate size 2,048, trained with the physical degradation collator at a masking rate of 
8% of characters. 
Since each token corresponds to exactly one character, there are no tokenisation boundary 
effects at lacuna edges: every masked position corresponds to precisely one absent character, and 
the model predicts exactly one character per position. This alignment between the restoration 
target and the model's output unit is the key structural advantage over BPE-based approaches. 

4.3 Auxiliary Classification 
Building on the metadata described in Section 3.2, we train two auxiliary classifiers — 
for genre category and for conventional date — on top of the frozen embeddings produced by 
each of the five models. The classifiers are not trained end-to-end with the language models; 
instead, we extract fixed representations and train a lightweight probe on top of them. 
For each model and each task, we train a linear probe: a single linear layer preceded by 
layer normalisation and dropout, applied to the pooled sequence representation. For BERT-based 
models the [CLS] token is used as the document representation; for RoFormer and DualEmbLM, 
the mean of non-padding token representations is used. Two versions of the input are evaluated: 
embeddings extracted from the restored text (target) and from the masked text with lacunae 
replaced by [GAP] tokens (masked), allowing us to measure the degradation in classification 
quality when the model operates on damaged rather than complete text. 
Category classification is framed as a four-class problem (letters, records, religious, 
other) trained with weighted cross-entropy loss to address class imbalance (696 / 330 / 171 / 45 
instances respectively). The probe is optimised with Adam (lr=1e-3) for 50 epochs; the best 
checkpoint is selected by macro-F1 on the validation set. Performance is reported as accuracy 
and macro-F1 on the test set, accompanied by a per-class confusion matrix. 
Date classification follows the Aeneas approach: rather than predicting a single bin, the 
probe outputs a probability distribution over nine 50-year bins. The training target for each 
manuscript is a uniform distribution over the bins overlapping its conventional date interval, and 
the loss is KL divergence between the predicted and target distributions. The evaluation metric is 
the distance in years between the predicted mean date — the expected value of the predicted 
distribution — and the ground-truth interval, with a distance of zero awarded when the predicted 
mean falls within the interval. 
All five models are evaluated on the same stratified train/val/test split (70/15/15), with 
stratification by category. Records with mixed-category annotations (containing //) are excluded 
from category classification; records without a parseable date interval are excluded from date 
classification. 
5. Evaluation 
5.1 Metrics 
All models are evaluated on Test A and Test B using three complementary metrics. 
Hit@k measures whether the correct answer appears among the model's top-k predictions (k ∈ 
{1, 5, 20}); it is computed per character (fine-tuned and DualEmbLM) or per token (RoFormer), 

and additionally at the span level for from-scratch models. Character error rate (CER) is the 
normalised Levenshtein distance between the predicted and target string. Perplexity (PPL) 
measures overall language modelling quality on the evaluation split. For from-scratch models, 
we additionally report span-level macro-CER, averaged uniformly across span lengths 1–10 
following Aeneas; span-level Hit@k requires the entire reconstructed lacuna to match the target 
exactly. 
The best checkpoint for each model is selected by Hit@1 on Test B in the corresponding 
prediction mode: character-level Hit@1 for fine-tuned models evaluated in character mode and 
for DualEmbLM, token-level Hit@1 for fine-tuned models evaluated in token mode and for 
RoFormer. 
To reduce per-epoch evaluation time, the number of assessed gap positions is capped at 
5,000 per test set. For token-level models, this limit is not reached nor for BERTislav and 
mBERT on both Test A (2,373–3,594 positions depending on tokenisation) and Test B (3,941 
and 4,668 positions respectively); however, it is applied for ModernBERT on Test B. For 
character-level models, the limit is applied on both test sets for all three models. 
5.2 Fine-tuned Models 
5.2.1 Zero-shot 
All three models are evaluated without fine-tuning under character-level constrained 
decoding. Performance is uniformly weak, which is expected: models originally trained to 
predict subword tokens perform poorly when restricted to single-character prediction. BERTislav 
achieves marginally higher results on Test A, consistent with its pre-training domain partially 
overlapping with the target.  
Notably, on Test B Hit@1 scores are higher for all three models. ModernBERT achieves 
the highest Test B Hit@1 (0.069) and the lowest CER (0.911) among the three, indicating an 
architectural advantage even in the zero-shot setting.

6. Limitations & Future Work 
6.1 Limitations 
Masking rate mismatch. As discussed in Section 3.4, both token-level and 
character-level models are trained with a masking rate of 8%, chosen to match the empirical 
share of editorial reconstructions in Test B. However, this rate operates at different granularities: 
for character-level models, 8% of characters are masked, directly matching the target 
distribution; for token-level ones, 8% of BPE tokens are masked, each of which may span 
several characters, resulting in a substantially higher effective character-level masking rate. This 
discrepancy is an inherent limitation of applying a uniform masking rate across architectures 

with different tokenisation granularities, and is acknowledged as such in the interpretation of 
results. 
BPE boundary effects. When a lacuna boundary falls within a BPE token, the entire 
token is masked during Test B evaluation, causing the model to lose access to characters that lie 
outside the bracketed span but within the same token. This systematically widens the lacunae and 
constitutes a mild but consistent disadvantage for token-level models compared to 
character-level models, which mask exactly the characters specified by the editorial markup. 
Dataset size. The birchbark and epigraphic components of the training corpus are 
relatively small (approximately 30,000 word tokens combined), and the models rely heavily on 
related but stylistically distinct sources to compensate. The degree to which this domain 
mismatch affects restoration quality on Old Novgorodian texts specifically remains an open 
question. 
Normalisation coverage. The current normalisation pipeline does not handle digits, 
which remain in the training data as surface forms. Digit tokens are consequently included in the 
model vocabulary and may appear among top-k predictions for masked positions — a behaviour 
that is linguistically unmotivated for lacuna restoration. Future work should extend the 
normalisation to exclude or replace numeral forms. 
6.2 Future Work 
Several directions present themselves naturally. First, incorporating document-level 
thematic tags (e.g. LEGAL, CHURCH) as conditioning tokens — analogous to the CTX tags 
used in the data augmentation pipeline — could allow the model to leverage genre-level 
regularities in restoration. Second, extending the from-scratch architectures with a length 
prediction head, following Aeneas, would enable restoration in the more realistic setting where 
lacuna boundaries are uncertain. Third, evaluation on a larger and more balanced test set, 
stratified by lacuna length and document genre, would provide a more reliable basis for 
cross-model comparison. 
7. Conclusions 
This work presents a systematic comparison of four approaches to automatic lacuna 
restoration in Old Novgorodian birchbark manuscripts, spanning fine-tuned multilingual 
encoders and architectures trained from scratch on a purpose-built Old Russian and Old Church 
Slavonic corpus. 
The principal finding concerns the from-scratch architectures. Character-level 
tokenisation substantially outperforms subword tokenisation in this setting: DualEmbLM, which 

combines character-level input with word-level context, achieves a Test B character Hit@1 of 
46.6% and a span Hit@1 of 22.2%, compared to 15.8% and 1.7% respectively for RoFormer, 
which operates on BPE tokens. The BPE boundary artefact — whereby token boundaries do not 
align with lacuna boundaries — is identified as the primary structural cause of this gap, 
manifesting in a span macro-CER exceeding 1.0 for RoFormer. 
Fine-tuned models evaluated in character-level mode perform substantially worse than 
either from-scratch architecture, with the best result of 0.147 for ModernBERT remaining well 
below DualEmbLM's 0.466. This reflects the mismatch between subword pre-training and 
character-level prediction that domain fine-tuning only partially alleviates. When the same 
fine-tuned models are evaluated in token-level mode — predicting subword units rather than 
individual characters — performance improves markedly: ModernBERT achieves a Test B token 
Hit@1 of 0.277, BERTislav 0.204, and mBERT 0.271. However, these results are not directly 
comparable to character-level metrics, as they are computed over different prediction units and 
different test set compositions. Within the token-level evaluation, ModernBERT consistently 
outperforms BERTislav and mBERT across all metrics and both test sets, confirming that recent 
architectural improvements to the bidirectional encoder paradigm transfer effectively to 
low-resource historical language modelling despite the absence of domain-specific pre-training. 
The auxiliary probing experiments show that all five models encode genre and temporal 
information to a degree recoverable by a linear classifier. BERTislav achieves the highest 
category macro-F1, consistent with its domain-specific pre-training, while the target–masked 
degradation is most pronounced for ModernBERT, indicating greater sensitivity to input 
corruption despite its superior restoration performance.
